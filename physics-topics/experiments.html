Basics of Physics - Experiments

What are Physics Experiments?

Experiments are the foundation of physics. They test theories, reveal new phenomena, and provide the evidence that shapes our understanding of the universe. From Galileo’s inclined planes to modern particle accelerators, experiments drive progress in physics.

Main thing to know:
Experiments validate theories and uncover the laws of nature.

Key Equations in MOT Physics

\[ F_{\text{scatt}} = \hbar k \cdot \frac{\Gamma}{2} \cdot \frac{I/I_{\text{sat}}}{1 + I/I_{\text{sat}} + \left( \frac{2\Delta}{\Gamma} \right)^2} \]
Scattering force: describes the momentum transfer from photons to atoms, where \( \hbar k \) is photon momentum, \( \Gamma \) the natural linewidth, \( I \) the laser intensity, \( I_{\text{sat}} \) the saturation intensity, and \( \Delta \) the detuning.
\[ F_{\text{MOT}} \approx -\kappa x \]
Restoring force in a MOT: atoms experience a position-dependent force proportional to displacement \( x \), with spring constant \( \kappa \) set by the magnetic field gradient and laser parameters.

Why MOTs Matter

MOTs enable the study of atoms at ultracold temperatures, opening the door to quantum simulation, atomic clocks, Bose-Einstein condensates, and precision tests of fundamental physics. They are the experimental workhorse for trapping and cooling neutral atoms.
