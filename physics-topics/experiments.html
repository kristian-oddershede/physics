Basics of Physics - Experiments

What are Physics Experiments?

Experiments are the foundation of physics. They test theories, reveal new phenomena, and provide the evidence that shapes our understanding of the universe. From Galileo’s inclined planes to modern particle accelerators, experiments drive progress in physics.

Main thing to know:
Experiments validate theories and uncover the laws of nature.

Famous Physics Experiments

Double-Slit Experiment

Demonstrates the wave-particle duality of light and matter. When particles pass through two slits, they form an interference pattern, showing wave-like behavior.

\[ I(\theta) \propto \cos^2\left(\frac{\pi d \sin \theta}{\lambda}\right) \]
Interference intensity \( I(\theta) \) depends on slit separation \( d \), wavelength \( \lambda \), and angle \( \theta \).

Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment

Measured the charge of the electron by balancing gravitational and electric forces on tiny oil droplets.

\[ qE = mg \]
Balance of electric force \( qE \) and gravitational force \( mg \) on a droplet.

Cavendish Experiment

Determined the gravitational constant \( G \) by measuring the tiny force between lead spheres.

\[ F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \]
Gravitational force between masses \( m_1 \) and \( m_2 \) separated by distance \( r \).

Modern Experimental Physics

Today, experiments range from tabletop optics setups to massive international collaborations. Examples include:

  • Particle collisions at CERN’s Large Hadron Collider
  • Laser interferometry in LIGO detecting gravitational waves
  • Quantum computing experiments with trapped ions
  • Space-based telescopes measuring cosmic background radiation
