Basics of Physics - Experiments

What are Physics Experiments?

Experiments are the foundation of physics. They aim to disprove theories, and the theories that stand the test of time and our contious scientific efforts - are the theories we consider to be true (or at least our best current theories).

Main thing to know:
Experiments try to disprove theories in under to test the limits of theories.

An example of the methods

In the following, there are brief descriptions and images from an experimental bachelor project. It aimed to study the possibility of trapping neutral atoms through a special trap called a MOT (Magneto Optical Trap). It uses lasers and a magnetic field to do so.

Theory - Key Equations for atomic trapping

Very briefly are here some key equations, that are essential for why trapping of neutral atoms may be possible - if we trust the theory.
\[ F_{\text{scatt}} = \hbar k \cdot \frac{\Gamma}{2} \cdot \frac{I/I_{\text{sat}}}{1 + I/I_{\text{sat}} + \left( \frac{2\Delta}{\Gamma} \right)^2} \]
Scattering force: describes the momentum transfer from photons to atoms, where \( \hbar k \) is photon momentum, \( \Gamma \) the natural linewidth, \( I \) the laser intensity, \( I_{\text{sat}} \) the saturation intensity, and \( \Delta \) the detuning.
\[ F_{\text{MOT}} \approx -\kappa x \]
Restoring force in a MOT: atoms experience a position-dependent force proportional to displacement \( x \), with spring constant \( \kappa \) set by the magnetic field gradient and laser parameters.

Why MOTs Matter

MOTs enable the study of atoms at ultracold temperatures, opening the door to further experiments with: quantum simulations, atomic clocks, Bose-Einstein condensates, and precision tests of fundamental physics. They are an essential experimental tool for trapping and cooling of neutral atoms.

Methods

When we have a certain tehory, we can set up an experiment to test its validity. Designing the experiments themselves are not an easy task. It requires careful consideration and precision is important if we want to later make conclusions based on our results.

Data collection

When the experiment is set up, we have to collect data. Already here on the image, we see exactly what we would expect - a little glowing dot in the middle of our glass cell. This glowing dot are the trapped atoms.

Results

The result is what we actually measure, and that is very rarely the phenomena itself. We can't directly "measure" the atoms, but we can't measure the light that they emit. That is an important distinction in many physics experiments.

Heat map for the intensity of the trapped rubidium atoms.

Analysis

When we have our results, they are rarely useful directly, without being processed and the experiment repeated. Conclusion

Based on our data analysis, we can examine wether what we observe was similar to what we predicted from our theory. In this case we did indeed trap atoms, and they behaved as expected when we changed different parameters, so we could not disprove the theory. Many others have tried as well, and failed to disproved, so our modern understanding of how atoms interact with lasers, and how we can trap them - is generally cosidered to be true today.
