What is Mechanics?

Mechanics is the branch of physics that deals with the motion of objects and the forces that cause or change this motion. It forms the foundation for understanding how everyday objects behave as well as how celestial bodies move in space. The study of mechanics can be divided into classical mechanics, which focuses on macroscopic objects moving at speeds much less than the speed of light, and relativistic mechanics, which applies when objects move close to the speed of light.

Main thing to know:
Newton’s Laws of Motion govern how forces affect motion, and they provide the framework to analyze and predict the behavior of physical systems.

Key Equations in Mechanics

F = m a
Newton's Second Law: Force equals mass times acceleration.

This fundamental law states that the acceleration a of an object is proportional to the net force F applied to it and inversely proportional to its mass m.

p = m v
Momentum: the product of mass and velocity.

Momentum p is a vector quantity representing the quantity of motion an object has. It is conserved in isolated systems, meaning total momentum before and after interactions remains the same.

W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(\theta)
Work done by a force F moving an object through displacement d at an angle θ.

Work is the energy transferred by a force. When the force is in the direction of motion, it increases the object’s kinetic energy.

K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2
Kinetic Energy: energy of motion.

The kinetic energy K of a mass moving at velocity v shows how much energy is stored due to its motion.

U = m g h
Gravitational Potential Energy: energy due to an object’s height h.

This is the energy stored by an object as a result of its vertical position in a gravitational field (with g representing gravitational acceleration).

Further Details

Newton’s Laws of Motion

  1. First law (Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net external force.
  2. Second law: The net force on an object equals its mass times acceleration (F = m a).
  3. Third law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Conservation Laws

Conservation of momentum and energy are central principles in mechanics. They allow us to predict the outcomes of collisions and other interactions without knowing all the forces in detail.

Types of Mechanics

  • Statics: Study of bodies at rest or in equilibrium.
  • Kinematics: Describes motion without regard to forces.
  • Dynamics: Studies forces and their effects on motion.
